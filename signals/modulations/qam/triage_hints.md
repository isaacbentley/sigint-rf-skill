# Triage Hints: QAM (Quadrature Amplitude Modulation)

These hints help the LLM identify and verify QAM (QAM-16/QAM-64) signals from statistical reports generated by `triage_iq.py`.

---

## 🔍 Key Spectral & Statistical Indicators

### 1. High PAPR (Peak-to-Average Power Ratio)
* Unlike FSK and PSK, QAM deliberately modulates the carrier amplitude. Some constellation points are very close to zero power, while corner points represent high power.
* **QAM-16**: Measured PAPR is typically **$5.0\text{ dB to } 7.0\text{ dB}$**.
* **QAM-64**: Measured PAPR is typically **$7.0\text{ dB to } 9.0\text{ dB}$**.
* A high PAPR during active bursts, combined with a flat-topped PSD, strongly points to QAM (or OFDM, which is a collection of QAM subcarriers).

### 2. Amplitude Variance (Std/Mean)
* The standard deviation of the envelope magnitude normalized by the mean is moderately high:
  - **Normalized Std/Mean**: Typically **$0.25\text{ to } 0.45$**.
  - This is lower than OOK (where Std/Mean is $>0.5$ due to complete carrier suppression), but higher than FSK ($<0.05$) and PSK ($<0.15$).

---

## ⏱ Key Temporal Indicators

* QAM is strictly used in high-speed digital communications, meaning it is bursty and packetized (like Wi-Fi packets).
* High-order QAM requires a very high SNR ($> 20\text{ dB}$ for QAM-16, $> 28\text{ dB}$ for QAM-64) to be decodable. If the SNR measured by `triage_iq.py` is low ($< 15\text{ dB}$), QAM is highly unlikely.

---

## 🧬 Autocorrelation Periodicities

* Like PSK, RRC filtering in QAM causes amplitude variations matching symbol transitions.
* Autocorrelation of the envelope magnitude will show clear peaks at the symbol duration lag $T_{sym}$.
* Identifying this symbol clock lag helps the receiver determine the correct timing offsets to sample the $I$ and $Q$ grids.
