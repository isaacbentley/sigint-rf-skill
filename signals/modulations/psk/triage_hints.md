# Triage Hints: PSK (Phase Shift Keying)

These hints help the LLM identify and verify PSK (BPSK/QPSK) signals from statistical reports generated by `triage_iq.py`.

---

## 🔍 Key Spectral & Statistical Indicators

### 1. Medium PAPR (with Pulse Shaping)
* Unfiltered PSK has a constant envelope (low PAPR). However, to limit occupied bandwidth, almost all real systems use Root-Raised Cosine (RRC) filtering.
* RRC filtering causes the signal amplitude to drop during phase transitions (especially $180^\circ$ phase reversals).
* The measured PAPR of a filtered PSK signal is typically **$2.0\text{ dB to } 5.0\text{ dB}$**.

### 2. Phase Histogram Peaks (The Primary Identifier)
* If the phase angle of the active complex samples is computed:
  $$\phi[n] = \angle(x[n])$$
  and plotted as a histogram, PSK signals exhibit sharp, discrete clusters:
  - **BPSK**: **Two distinct peaks** separated by $180^\circ$ ($\pi$ radians).
  - **QPSK**: **Four distinct peaks** separated by $90^\circ$ ($\pi/2$ radians).
* *Note*: If carrier offset is present, the phase constellation rotates, which blurs the raw phase histogram into a flat distribution. Frequency offset must be corrected before phase histogram analysis.

---

## ⏱ Key Temporal Indicators

* PSK is typically packetized, with duty cycles depending on the protocol.
* If looking at the raw amplitude envelope, a filtered PSK signal appears as a continuous block of power, but with periodic, tiny amplitude dips corresponding to the symbol boundaries (where phase changes occur).

---

## 🧬 Autocorrelation Periodicities

* The autocorrelation of the envelope magnitude of a filtered PSK signal will show weak peaks at the symbol duration lag $T_{sym}$.
* This is due to the periodic amplitude dips at phase transitions.
* Identifying these minor periodic dips helps determine the symbol rate before phase recovery is run.
