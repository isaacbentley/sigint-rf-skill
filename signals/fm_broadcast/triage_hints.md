# Triage Hints: WBFM — Wideband FM Radio Broadcasting

These hints help identify and verify Wideband FM broadcast signals from spectral analysis, waterfall displays, or statistical reports generated by `triage_iq.py`.

---

## 🔍 Key Spectral Indicators

### 1. Frequency Band
* FM broadcast operates exclusively in the **88–108 MHz** VHF band worldwide.
* If your capture center frequency falls within or overlaps this range, WBFM is the **most likely** signal present.
* Stations are spaced at 200 kHz (Americas) or 100 kHz (Europe/Australia) intervals — e.g., 88.1, 88.3, 88.5 MHz (US odd spacing).

### 2. Occupied Bandwidth (OBW)
* Each FM station occupies approximately **200 kHz** of bandwidth — far wider than any other VHF signal in this band.
* The spectral shape is a **broad, rounded hump** centered on the station frequency, distinct from the sharp spikes of narrowband signals.
* During loud audio passages, the spectrum fills the full ±75 kHz deviation. During silence or quiet passages, it narrows noticeably (a telltale of FM — the bandwidth is content-dependent).

### 3. Spectral Shape & Subcarriers
* The raw RF spectrum shows the classic FM "hill" shape — no discrete carrier spike (unlike AM).
* After FM demodulation, the **composite baseband PSD** reveals the definitive internal structure:
  - A strong audio band from 0–15 kHz (mono L+R).
  - A **sharp spectral line at exactly 19 kHz** — the stereo pilot tone (the single most diagnostic feature).
  - A mirrored sideband structure from 23–53 kHz (stereo L−R DSB-SC around 38 kHz).
  - A small bump at 57 kHz ±2 kHz (RDS subcarrier) if RDS is present.

---

## ⏱ Key Temporal Indicators

### 1. Continuous Transmission
* FM broadcast stations transmit **24/7 continuously** — they never go off the air during normal operation.
* This is a key differentiator from bursty signals (ACARS, AIS, pagers) or periodic signals (radar, beacons).
* Any capture in the 88–108 MHz range will show **constant signal presence** at occupied channels.

### 2. Amplitude Envelope
* FM is a **constant-envelope** modulation — the instantaneous amplitude does not vary with the audio content.
* The IQ magnitude should be approximately constant (flat envelope) for a clean FM signal.
* **PAPR is very low (~0 dB)** for a single FM station — this is a strong discriminator against AM, SSB, or OFDM signals.

### 3. Audio Content
* After FM demodulation, the baseband contains recognizable **audio content** — music, speech, or silence.
* This is the ultimate "sanity check": if you demodulate WFM and hear a radio station, it's FM broadcast.
* Typical audio bandwidth: 30 Hz – 15 kHz (noticeably better than AM broadcast's ~5 kHz).

---

## 🧬 Key Identification Heuristic

> **The "smoking gun" for FM broadcast: a ~200 kHz wide constant-envelope signal in the 88–108 MHz band. After FM demodulation, the composite baseband shows a sharp 19 kHz pilot tone spike. If the 19 kHz pilot is present, it is FM stereo broadcast — nothing else in the radio spectrum uses this exact signature.**

## ❌ Common False Positives / Confusable Signals

| Confused With | How to Distinguish |
|---|---|
| **Narrowband FM (NBFM) — land mobile, amateur** | NBFM occupies only 10–15 kHz (±5 kHz deviation) vs. 200 kHz for WBFM. NBFM appears as a thin spike compared to FM broadcast's broad hump. Also, NBFM operates outside 88–108 MHz (typically 136–174 or 400–470 MHz). |
| **DAB/DAB+ (Digital Audio Broadcasting)** | DAB is a 1.536 MHz wide flat-topped OFDM block in VHF Band III (174–240 MHz), well above the FM band. Rectangular spectral shape vs. FM's rounded hump. |
| **TV audio carriers (analog)** | Analog TV audio used FM but at different frequencies (typically 4.5 MHz offset from video carrier in VHF/UHF TV bands). Analog TV is now almost entirely defunct. |
| **HD Radio OFDM sidebands** | HD Radio OFDM appears as low-level "shoulders" flanking the analog FM signal at ±100–200 kHz. It accompanies — rather than replaces — the analog signal. If you see OFDM sidebands within 88–108 MHz, the central analog FM signal is still the primary. |
| **Aviation VOR (108–118 MHz)** | VOR operates just above the FM band (108–117.95 MHz). VOR has a 30 Hz AM reference tone and a 30 Hz FM variable tone on a 9960 Hz subcarrier — very different composite structure, no 19 kHz pilot. Much narrower bandwidth. |
| **Pager (POCSAG/FLEX) in VHF** | Pagers are narrowband FSK bursts (~25 kHz wide max). Bursty, not continuous. Much narrower and in different frequency ranges. |

## ✅ High-Confidence WBFM Identification Checklist

1. ☐ Center frequency falls within **88–108 MHz**
2. ☐ Occupied bandwidth is approximately **200 kHz** (±75 kHz deviation visible)
3. ☐ Signal is **continuous** (not bursty or periodic)
4. ☐ **Constant envelope** — PAPR near 0 dB for the isolated signal
5. ☐ FM demodulation produces **intelligible audio** (music, speech, silence)
6. ☐ Composite baseband PSD shows **19 kHz pilot tone** (stereo indicator)
7. ☐ Stereo L−R subcarrier visible at **23–53 kHz** (DSB-SC around 38 kHz)
8. ☐ RDS subcarrier visible at **57 kHz** (optional but common — confirms broadcast origin)
9. ☐ Station can be cross-referenced with local FM station database (e.g., radio-locator.com, fmscan.org)
